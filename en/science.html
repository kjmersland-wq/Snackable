The Forgetting Curve — and how we break it

German psychologist Hermann Ebbinghaus spent the 1880s memorising nonsense syllables and testing himself at regular intervals. What he discovered changed how we understand learning forever:

Without active review, we forget roughly 50–60% of new information within one hour, and up to 80% within 24 hours. After a week, almost everything is gone.

But Ebbinghaus discovered something else: every time you revisit information, it takes less time to relearn — and the memory lasts longer. That insight is the foundation of Spaced Repetition.

The Testing Effect — getting tested is learning

Henry Roediger III and Jeffrey Karpicke at Washington University published a 2006 study that shook the learning psychology field: students who tested themselves on material remembered dramatically more after one week than those who re-read it — even though the re-reading group spent more total time studying.

The mechanism is called retrieval practice: when the brain actively tries to recall information, the neural pathways associated with that information get stronger. Think of it like a path through a forest — the more you walk it, the clearer it becomes.

Spaced Repetition — the optimal review algorithm

Sebastian Leitner developed the Leitner system in 1972 as the first practical implementation of Spaced Repetition. Piotr Woźniak then refined it into the SM-2 algorithm in 1987, which became the foundation for Anki — and now for SnackAble Learn.

The principle: the review interval doubles with each successful attempt. Concepts you're shaky on get reviewed often; concepts you've nailed get reviewed rarely. The result is that you spend up to 90% less time reviewing, but retain just as much — because you're reviewing smart.

SnackAble Learn calculates an individually optimal review interval for each flashcard based on your actual performance history.

Four principles

Cognitive psychology in practice

SnackAble Learn is built on four well-documented principles from cognitive psychology and educational research.

01

Active Recall (Testing Effect)

Actively retrieving information from memory strengthens memory traces far more than passive re-exposure. Implemented via automatic quiz generation for every concept.

02

Spaced Repetition

Reviewing at optimal intervals — precisely when you're about to forget — produces dramatically better retention than massed practice (cramming everything at once).

03

Desirable Difficulty

Robert Bjork (UCLA): learning is most effective when it's slightly challenging. Questions that are too easy produce poor retention. SnackAble Learn calibrates difficulty automatically.

04

Chunking & Micro-learning

George Miller (1956): working memory holds roughly 7 ± 2 items at a time. SnackAble Learn breaks content into 3–5 minute modules to respect that cognitive limit.
